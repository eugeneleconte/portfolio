Project overview and background

Each year during my studies at ITEEM Centrale Lille, we were asked to work in teams on technical projects. Half a day a week was devoted to these projects, not including all the hours spent working on them after class, at weekends or during lunch breaks. In the second year, as a team of four students, we collaborated with a company for the first time. Among the projects on offer, we chose to work with Espace Ombrage, a specialist in shade sails since 2004, which places customer satisfaction at the heart of its business. Espace Ombrage's aim is to offer affordable products with a focus on quality.

One of the challenges facing Espace Ombrage is the transport and logistics involved in delivering the sails. These products, often of considerable size, require “out-of-class” parcels, which generates additional costs. This is particularly true for motorized shade sails, whose sails automatically retract into a heavy, cumbersome metal roll-up boom. Jérôme, in charge of the project at Espace Ombrage, then suggested we develop a motorized roller boom made up of several parts, which could be easily dismantled and adapted.

This solution would offer several advantages :

  • Reduced package size : individual boom parts can be stored side by side, reducing package length.
  • User autonomy : thanks to the tutorials on offer and the ease with which sails can be dismantled, users would be able to remove or reinstall their shade sail without the need for a specialist third party.
  • Cost reduction through standardization : the different parts of the boom would be adaptable to different sail formats. For example, by offering standardized one-meter sections, these could be used for two- or three-meter sails. This would reduce costs, both in terms of production and logistics, thanks to the resizing of packages.

If I could remember just one thing :

Make it real. We managed to deliver a real-scale, tangible final prototype, which gave us a better understanding of our product, what worked and what didn't. Since then, I've been trying to design a concrete prototype as soon as possible ( and as fas as I can!) to visualize my final solutions more easily.
