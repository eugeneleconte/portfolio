Project overview and background

For this fourth-year project at ITEEM, we had to choose an electrical appliance from among those available to our class. Working in teams of 4 students, we had to find different ways to reconceptualize the product using a responsible approach: life cycle analysis, functional study of the product, study of the materials used... We then had to work on several aspects in parallel. We opted for a blower/vacuum cleaner, an electric tool used mainly for moving tree leaves, which can suck them up or disperse them by blowing them away.

When we took it to pieces and looked at the way it had been designed, we noticed that certain aspects of the product had been conceptualized with a certain sense of responsibility. For example, the blower/vacuum can be easily dismantled and reassembled. Defective parts can therefore be easily replaced, rather than having to buy the product all over again. We've also noticed that RYOBI's after-sales service (the brand of the blower/vacuum) seems to be very good, making it easy to buy spare parts.

On our side, here are the elements we identified to support the product's socio-environmental responsibilities :

RYOBI blower/vacuum to be redesigned
  • Product weight reduction: the use of lighter materials reduces user tiredness and tension.
  • Eco-responsible materials: Currently made from nylon, we can imagine a redesign using recycled and/or recyclable materials that don't compromise the product's durability. With further research, we believe that the use of recycled nylon would be a solution.
  • Energy consumption : The system uses a battery that pollutes a lot. We wondered whether the blower/vacuum could be powered by electric wiring. However, for reasons of usability, the product would no longer be practical to use, given that it is mainly used outdoors. We also noted that the product lacks a notch to manage motor power and therefore energy consumption. Nevertheless, adding functionality to a product also implies additional manufacturing costs and new materials, and is therefore not necessarily a responsible redesign idea.
