Project overview and background

First large-scale project of my iteemian curriculum: in a predefined team, we chose a small, everyday appliance and adapted it so that a disabled person could use it. We decided on a beer tap adapted for blind people. First, we had to validate that blind or partially-sighted people would indeed have difficulty serving themselves a beer from the fountain. We also had to make sure that no existing innovation was sufficiently developed to meet the needs of our target group. We therefore met dozens of blind people to verify these two points and together find a truly adequate solution. For example, we met the Blind Soccer Team of the Racing Club of Lens, a moment of exchange rich in ideas and inspiration, since the players we met were huge fans of beer! They explained, among other things, their desire to be able to serve themselves a beer “like everyone else”, using a tapping machine similar to those on the market.

Next, we had to look more closely at the technical aspects of the project: how could we deliver the innovations we had imagined? Are they feasible? How could users use them “like everyone else”? As shown in fig.1, we then chose to add various control transmitters and wheels: a loudspeaker to give information when a user presses the information button, and two wheels to adjust quantity and temperature, essential for managing beer quality.

Next, we had to look more closely at the technical aspects of the project: how could we deliver the innovations we had imagined? Are they feasible? How could users use them “like everyone else”? As shown in fig.1, we then chose to add various control transmitters and wheels: a loudspeaker to give information when a user presses the information button, and two wheels to adjust quantity and temperature, essential for managing beer quality. To help blind people find their way around the filler, we decided that each wheel and button would have a different texture. Indeed, you may notice that, on the fig.2, the blue (1) and green (2) wheels do not have the same number of steps and are of different sizes. The colors symbolize the different textures. The information button (3) and wheels could be designed in three different materials. In the end, the people with whom we had regular exchanges during the project confirmed that our solution would be an answer to their desire to use a beer tapping machine. Some of them even pre-ordered a suitable beer tap in advance, just in case the project came to reality!
